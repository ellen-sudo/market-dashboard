Market Analysis










The Wake County market in February 2026 is giving buyers something they haven't had in a while: time. Median days on market climbed to 47 days, up from just 28 days last year — a significant shift that means you have more room to evaluate your options carefully before committing. With 1,745 new listings coming to market and pendings rising to 1,359, there is healthy inventory to work with. This is not a market where you need to panic-buy, but it is also not one where you can be passive about the right home.


Here's what the data doesn't fully capture: the market is bifurcating sharply. While the average home is sitting longer, well-priced and well-presented homes are still generating immediate competition. A modest home in the $800k range in Cary went multiple offers its first weekend — not because it was extraordinary, but because it was convenient, priced right, and had no red flags. Meanwhile, a beautiful home with a significant drainage issue sat untouched despite being priced well. Buyers today are experienced and discerning. They want move-in ready. One meaningful flaw can change a home's entire market position — which is actually good news for buyers who do their homework.


The practical takeaway: use the extra time the market is giving you wisely. Inspect carefully, ask questions about anything that concerns you, and don't let a beautiful kitchen distract you from a problematic yard. Sellers are still receiving 98.3% of list price on solid homes, so there's limited room to negotiate on properties without issues. On homes that have been sitting, there's more room to work. Contact Harmony Realty — we know which homes fall into which category before you ever set foot inside.







The Wake County market in February 2026 is sending sellers a clear message: the days of every home selling quickly regardless of condition are over. Median days on market has risen to 47 days from 28 days last year — nearly double — and expired listings have jumped from 200 to 326. Those expired listings are not bad luck. They are the predictable result of overpricing, under-preparation, or a significant flaw that buyers in this market simply are not willing to overlook. The sellers who are winning right now are the ones who understand this.


What's striking from the ground level is how binary the outcomes are. A modest, well-presented home in the $800k range in Cary went multiple offers its first weekend — not because it was special, but because it was clean, priced accurately, and had no red flags. A beautiful home with a drainage issue and wet yard sat, despite being priced well. Buyers today are older, more experienced, and unwilling to take on problems. They don't want projects. They are not going to overlook a sump pump running constantly or drainage easements across the backyard, no matter how beautiful the kitchen is. One flaw can cost you weeks on market and a significant price reduction.


The opportunity is still real — sellers are averaging 98.3% of list price and 11.3% of homes are still closing above asking. But those outcomes belong to sellers who prepare properly and price honestly from day one. Overpriced homes are being ignored, not negotiated on. If you're thinking about selling, contact Harmony Realty before you do anything else. We'll tell you exactly what your home is worth in this market and what, if anything, needs to be addressed before you list.







The Wake County residential real estate market in February 2026 is undergoing a meaningful shift — and the headline numbers only tell part of the story. Median days on market rose to 47 days from 28 days last year, a 68% increase that signals buyers are taking more time and being more selective. New listings increased modestly to 1,745 from 1,676, while new pendings jumped to 1,359 from 1,195 — a seemingly contradictory combination that reveals a market sorting itself: more buyers are active, but they are concentrating their attention on a narrower slice of inventory.


The numbers that demand attention are expired listings and homes going under contract in under 10 days — sitting side by side in the same market. Expired listings climbed from 200 to 326 year over year. At the same time, 517 homes went under contract in less than 10 days. These are not two different markets — they are the same market, where condition and pricing determine everything. A home with a significant flaw, even a beautiful and well-priced one, is being passed over entirely. A clean, well-presented home with no red flags is still generating competition. The gap between those two outcomes has never been wider in recent memory.


Sellers are averaging 98.3% of list price at closing — down slightly from 98.9% last year — and 11.3% of homes are still closing above asking, compared to 16% the prior year. These are not alarming numbers, but they reflect a market that is normalizing after years of extreme seller conditions. For buyers, this is a window of opportunity to be more thoughtful and deliberate than was possible 18 months ago. For sellers, it is a wake-up call that preparation and honest pricing are no longer optional. The Triangle market remains healthy and active — but it is rewarding professionalism and punishing complacency.
